02 — Getting Around

Rent an ATV.
See everything.

Skip the taxis and tour buses. Renting an ATV is the single best decision you'll make on this island. You'll reach beaches, villages, and viewpoints that most visitors never find — on your own schedule, with the Aegean wind in your face.

Fira
Oia
Akrotiri
Red Beach
Perissa
  • Start before 9am — beat the heat and the tour groups, and you'll have the roads largely to yourself.
  • Bring cash. Most ATV rentals near Fira and Kamari are cash-only. Budget €35–50 per day.
  • Helmets are legally required and non-negotiable. The mountain roads have real exposure.
  • Fill the tank before leaving Fira. Petrol stations become sparse once you head south.
  • The full loop takes 4–5 hours at a relaxed pace with stops. Leave by 8am to finish before the midday heat.
  • Lock your ATV at every stop — petty theft is rare but not unheard of in peak season.
⚠ Caution — Roads into Oia

The final stretch into Oia narrows sharply. Pedestrians, donkeys, and delivery vehicles share the lane — sometimes simultaneously. Slow to a crawl, keep left, and never attempt to pass on blind corners. If the road feels too tight, park at the village outskirts and walk in. It's worth it either way.

03 — The Beaches

Black sand, red cliffs,
open water.

Santorini's beaches are unlike anything else in the Aegean. Volcanic geology gives you black and red sands, dramatic cliff backdrops, and water with an almost unreal clarity. Each beach has its own character — here's how to read them.

Volcanic

Red Beach

Akrotiri  ·  5 min from the ruins

Towering crimson cliffs plunge straight into electric-blue water. The approach — a short 10-minute scramble along a cliff path — is half the spectacle. Arrive before 10am; after that the crowds arrive. Sunbeds for hire on the left side.

Swimming

Perissa

Southeast coast  ·  Long black-sand strip

Three kilometres of fine black volcanic sand, excellent tavernas right on the shore, and calm water make this the best all-day beach for mixing food, swimming, and people-watching without feeling overrun.

Swimming

Perivolos

South of Perissa  ·  Beach club territory

Perissa's quieter, more polished neighbour. Sun loungers, cocktails, and ambient DJs in the late afternoon attract a crowd looking for something curated. The swimming is excellent and the vibe skews upscale without being inaccessible.

Scenic

Vlychada

South coast  ·  Almost no tourists

Eroded white pumice cliffs carved into alien formations — one of the most photographically surreal spots on the island. Almost nobody comes here. No vendors, no umbrellas, no sound but the waves. Bring everything. The reward is genuine solitude.

Scenic

White Beach

Near Red Beach  ·  Boat access recommended

Accessible only by water taxi from Red Beach (€5 each way) or a demanding cliff scramble. Pale cliffs, turquoise water, rarely more than a dozen visitors. Worth the logistical effort entirely.

04 — Amoudi Bay

For the brave.

The cliff jump
below Oia.

Two hundred steps below Oia village, Amoudi Bay sits tucked against the base of the caldera cliffs — small, salt-scented, and almost entirely unknown to the tour-bus crowd who congregate above for the sunset. It's one of Santorini's most genuinely local experiences, and the jumping rocks make it one of the island's most exhilarating.

The jump is off a flat rock shelf on the right side of the bay, approximately 8–10 metres above the surface. Local kids do it in flip-flops. Scout your entry and exit first — the volcanic rocks approaching the water are sharp — but the jump itself is clean and straightforward. Come at golden hour, when the day-trippers have dispersed and the cliffs turn amber above you.

  1. Descend the 200 steps from Oia village — follow signs for Ammoudi. Allow 15 minutes down, 20 back up. It's steep.
  2. Walk to the right side of the bay, past the fishing boats, to the flat rock platform at the water's edge.
  3. Watch a few locals jump first to identify the clean water beneath. Confirm there's no boat traffic before you go.
  4. Wear shoes you don't mind getting wet all the way to the edge — the approach rocks are sharp volcanic stone. Remove them just before the jump.
  5. After: walk directly to Dimitris or Sunset Tavern on the waterfront. Order the grilled octopus. Have a cold Mythos. You've earned it.

Practical Details

  • Best time: 5–7pm — golden hour light, far fewer people
  • Height: approximately 8–10m off the main platform
  • Water: deep, clear, no hazards directly below the jump
  • Access: walk down from Oia village, follow signs to Ammoudi
  • Footwear: shoes that can get wet — the approach rocks are sharp
  • Current: mild, but watch for small boat movement in the bay
  • After: Sunset Tavern or Dimitris — both right on the water, fresh octopus

    Oia  ·  Open caldera terrace

    The most celebrated table on the island. A tasting menu of elevated Cycladic cuisine served on a terrace cut into the caldera cliff, with the volcano as an unobstructed backdrop. Reserve weeks ahead — this is the meal you plan the trip around.

  • Selene

     Visit website ↗

    Pyrgos village  ·  Farm-to-table, Michelin recognition

    Away from the caldera circus, in the hilltop village of Pyrgos, Selene serves farm-to-table Greek cuisine with Michelin-level precision. Quieter, more intellectual, and often more satisfying than the view-heavy restaurants. The Assyrtiko wine list is outstanding.

  • Metaxy Mas

     TripAdvisor ↗

    Exo Gonia  ·  The locals' choice

    No caldera view, no Instagram presence — just exceptional Greek food at genuinely reasonable prices, served to a room full of locals and those in the know. The rabbit stifado and tomato fritters are non-negotiable. Ask for the table on the small terrace.

Casual & Local

    There will be a queue. Join it. Lucky's pita wraps — stuffed with grilled pork, tzatziki, tomato, and fries — are the best four euros you will spend in the Cyclades. Find it on the main street in Fira. Open until late.

    Santorini's most beloved breakfast institution. Arrive before 9am or face a wait. Greek yogurt with local thyme honey, fresh orange juice, perfect eggs. Simple, honest, and served with the warmth that makes you want to stay all morning. Do not skip this.

07 — Practical Notes

Everything you need
to know before you go.

◆

Best time to visit

Late May–June or September. Golden light, a fraction of August's crowds, and prices 20–30% lower. July and August are extraordinary but relentlessly hot and crowded — plan accordingly.

◆

Getting there

Fly into Santorini Airport (JTR) — 15 minutes from Fira. Olympic Air and Aegean fly frequently from Athens. The overnight ferry from Piraeus (5–8 hours) is a surprisingly enjoyable way to arrive.

◆

Currency & cash

Euro. Carry cash — ATV rentals, small tavernas, beach vendors, and water taxis are all cash-only. ATMs in Fira are reliable. Budget €50–80 per day in cash beyond accommodation.

◆

Sunsets — the honest advice

Oia sunset is iconic but the crowds are punishing. Imerovigli offers the same 180° view with a fraction of the people. If you do Oia, arrive two hours early and bring a drink.

◆

Book well ahead

Top hotels and restaurants like Lycabettus require 3–6 months notice in peak season. There is no walk-in at Canaves or Andronis in August. Plan this trip the way you'd plan a wedding.

◆

4 days is the right amount

Three nights gives you one full ATV day, two beach days, the cliff jump, a fine-dining dinner, and both the Oia and Imerovigli sunsets. Fewer than 3 nights feels rushed. More than 5 and you've seen everything twice.
